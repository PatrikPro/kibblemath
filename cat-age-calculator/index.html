How cat age conversion works

Cats, like dogs, sprint through their early development: a one-year-old cat is developmentally close to a 15-year-old human, and a two-year-old is around 24. From there, cats settle into a remarkably steady rhythm — roughly 4 human years per cat year — and unlike dogs, size barely matters, so one chart fits almost all cats:

6 months~10
1 year~15
2 years~24

10 years~56

The number is fun; the life stage is useful. Veterinary guidelines group cats as kittens (under 1), young adults (1–6), mature adults (7–10) and seniors (11+). Mature is when yearly bloodwork starts earning its keep; senior is when many vets recommend switching to twice-yearly checks, since kidney disease, thyroid problems and arthritis — the big three of cat aging — are far more manageable caught early.

Lifestyle shifts the odds more than genetics for most cats: indoor cats typically reach 13–17 years and quite often more, while regular outdoor access shortens the average considerably. Whatever the age, feeding to the right calorie target helps — our cat calorie calculator covers every life stage.

Not veterinary advice. Conversions and life stages are general guides. Your vet can tell you when your individual cat should move to senior-focused care.

Frequently asked questions

How old is my 5-year-old cat in human years?

About 36 — 24 for the first two years, plus 4 per year for the next three. At 5, a cat is a settled young adult in its prime.

When is a cat a senior?

From about 11 years, with 7–10 counted as "mature." Senior status is mainly a cue to increase check-up frequency and watch weight, thirst and mobility more closely.

Do big cats age faster like big dogs do?

No — the size effect that makes Great Danes age faster than Chihuahuas barely exists in domestic cats, which vary far less in size. One conversion chart fits nearly all breeds, with Maine Coons and similar giants only marginally different.
