How cat pregnancy timing works

Cats carry kittens for about 63–65 days, with anything from 58 to 70 considered normal. There's a biological quirk that makes the math friendlier than in dogs: cats are induced ovulators — the act of mating itself triggers ovulation within a day or two — so the mating date tracks conception closely. (It's also why a queen mated by several toms can deliver a litter with several fathers.)

The checkpoints along the way

Days 16–20: "pinking up" — nipples turn pinker and more prominent, often the first visible sign. A vet ultrasound can confirm pregnancy from around day 16. Weeks 4–6: appetite climbs; switch gradually to kitten food, which covers pregnancy's energy demands. Day 42+: an X-ray can count skeletons, so you know how many kittens to expect. Day 55: set up a quiet nesting box — queens pick their spot early, and it's better if it's yours. Day 58 onward: watch for labor signs — restlessness, nesting, calling, appetite loss, and milk in the days before.

When to call the vet during queening

Most cats deliver competently without help, but keep the number ready and call if: strong straining goes 30–60 minutes without a kitten, more than two hours pass between kittens while she's clearly still laboring, you see heavy bleeding or foul discharge, a kitten appears stuck, or day 70 passes with no labor at all. Quick calls are never wrong.

Breeding is a vet-partnership sport. This calculator organizes the timeline — it doesn't replace pregnancy confirmation, nutrition advice or a queening plan made with your veterinarian. And if this pregnancy wasn't planned: kittens can come as early as day 58, and a queen can conceive again astonishingly soon after delivery — worth a spay conversation.

Frequently asked questions

How long are cats pregnant?

About 63–65 days on average, normal range 58–70. Because mating induces ovulation, counting from the mating date works well — the window tightens further if a single mating date is known.

How can I tell my cat is pregnant early on?

Pinking up around days 16–20 is the classic first sign, alongside a gentle rise in appetite and affection. A vet can confirm by ultrasound from about day 16 and count kittens by X-ray from day 42.

What should I feed a pregnant cat?

From mid-pregnancy, transition to a complete kitten food and let her eat to appetite — energy needs climb steeply and stay high through nursing. Your vet can fine-tune amounts, especially for large litters.
