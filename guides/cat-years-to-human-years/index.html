Cat years to human years: the real conversion

Cats hit adulthood at lightning speed, then age with remarkable steadiness. Here's the conversion vets actually use — and why, unlike dogs, one chart fits nearly every cat.

The conversion chart

The pattern: 15 human years for the first cat year, 9 more for the second, then about 4 per year from there on:

Why one chart fits (almost) all cats

Dog owners need size classes because a Great Dane ages roughly twice as fast as a Chihuahua (we cover that oddity in the dog version of this guide). Domestic cats barely vary in size by comparison — a big Maine Coon is maybe three times a small Siamese, versus a fifty-fold weight spread in dogs — so the size-aging effect is negligible and a single conversion serves nearly every breed.

What the life stage is actually for

The human-age number is trivia; the stage drives decisions. From mature (7+), annual bloodwork starts earning its keep. From senior (11+), many vets recommend twice-yearly checks, because the big three of cat aging — chronic kidney disease, hyperthyroidism and arthritis — are dramatically more manageable caught early. Watch especially for increased thirst, weight change and reduced jumping; cats hide illness expertly, and those three leak through the act.

Lifespan-wise, indoor cats typically reach 13–17 years, late teens are increasingly routine, and the record — Creme Puff of Texas — stands at a scarcely believable 38. Keeping weight in the healthy zone is one of the few longevity levers you control; our cat calorie calculator is the place to start.

Not veterinary advice. Stages and conversions are general guides — your vet can judge when your individual cat should shift to senior-focused care.

Frequently asked questions

How old is a 5-year-old cat in human years?

About 36 — 24 for the first two years plus 4 per year for the next three. A 5-year-old cat is a settled adult in its prime.

When is a cat officially a senior?

From about 11 years, with 7–10 counted as "mature." It's a care milestone, not a decline sentence — the main change is check-up frequency.

Do indoor cats really live longer?

Substantially. Indoor cats average 13–17 years and often more, while regular outdoor access cuts the average sharply through traffic, fights and infectious disease. Enriched indoor life (or a secured "catio") captures most of both worlds.
