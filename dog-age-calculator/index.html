How dog age conversion actually works

The famous "multiply by seven" rule is neat, memorable — and wrong. Dogs sprint through development early: a one-year-old dog is roughly a 15-year-old human, and a two-year-old is around 24. After that, aging speed depends heavily on size — unusually among mammals, bigger dogs age faster.

The second number shown is the epigenetic clock: in 2020, researchers compared DNA methylation patterns in Labrador Retrievers and humans and derived the formula human age = 16 × ln(dog age) + 31. It captures the fast-early, slow-late shape of dog aging well — but it was built from a single breed, which is why we show both numbers rather than pretending one is exact.

Whichever number you prefer, the useful takeaway is your dog's life stage — puppy, adult or senior — because that's what should drive food choice (see our calorie calculator), exercise style and how often you book vet check-ups.

Frequently asked questions

Is one dog year really seven human years?

No. The 7:1 rule underestimates how fast puppies mature and ignores size entirely. The first dog year is worth roughly 15 human years, and each later year is worth 4 to 8 depending on breed size.

Why do large dogs age faster than small ones?

It's one of biology's odd inversions — across species, bigger animals live longer, but within dogs, selective breeding for size came with faster cellular aging and earlier onset of age-related disease. A Great Dane is a senior around 6–7; a Chihuahua often not until 10–11.

When should I switch to senior dog food or senior vet checks?

Use size as the guide: giant breeds around 6–7 years, large around 8, medium around 9, small around 10–11. Many vets recommend twice-yearly checks from senior age onward — worth discussing at your next visit.
